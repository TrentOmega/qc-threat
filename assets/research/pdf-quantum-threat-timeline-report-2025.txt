Summary
Several widely used cybersecurity protocols rely on computational challenges believed to be practically
unsolvable by classical computers. The advent of quantum computers that exploit quantum phenomena
at the microscopic level in a highly controlled manner would allow some of those challenges to be
overcome, posing severe cybersecurity risks.
To address this threat, new classical and quantum cryptographic techniques that are proven or
considered to be resistant to quantum attacks can be employed. Unfortunately, upgrading
cryptosystems to quantum-safe cryptography is a complex process. It involves developing and
implementing new hardware and software, establishing standards, and updating older systems, all while
maintaining a functioning cryptographic infrastructure and managing both past and current sensitive
data.
Because of the complexity and time
required by such a process, a successful
transition cannot take the form of
reactive, rushed crisis management; it
should be made an integral part of a
Figure 1 Mosca inequality
proactive technology lifecycle
management. The urgency of moving to quantum-resistant cryptography varies for each organization,
based on its security needs and risk tolerance. This urgency can be gauged using three primary factors:
• the shelf-life time: how many years the data must remain secure for;
• the migration time: how many years it will take to upgrade the systems guarding that data;
• the quantum threat time: the estimated time until potential adversaries gain access to quantum
computers of cryptographic significance.
The Mosca inequality (Figure 1) states that if the quantum threat time is less than the combined
duration of the shelf-life and migration times, organizations may fail to shield their assets from
quantum-enabled breaches. This document aims to offer an informed view on the proximity of the
quantum threat.
We shed light on the quantum threat timeline by sharing and analyzing the perspectives of
26 global experts from academia and industry, involved in all aspects of quantum
computing research, development, and commercialization.

The cyber risk posed by a quantum computer doesn’t simply lie in exposed
secrets. Suppose the quantum threat arrives before quantum-safe migrations
of systems are completed. In that case, integrity, availability, and operational
control across critical systems are at stake—that is, a systemic security and
operational failure, not just a confidentiality breach. On the other hand, it is
crucial to understand that adversaries do not need to remain inactive while
waiting for the availability of a CRQC: they can already intercept, duplicate,
and archive encrypted communications for future decryption—a strategy
known as “Harvest Now, Decrypt Later (HNDL).” This is accounted for in the
Mosca inequality, which considers the required shelf life of the data.

Governments are taking the quantum threat seriously by mandating a transition to Post-Quantum
Cryptography (PQC), for example, via the adoption of PQC algorithms and standards recently issued by
the US National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST). In Canada, a detailed roadmap for federal
IT systems was released in 2025: every government department must draft a PQC migration plan by
April 2026, with high-priority systems to switch to PQC already by the end of 2031. Along similar lines,
the European Commission has published a Coordinated PQC Transition Roadmap that requires all EU
member states to begin deploying post-quantum encryption by 2026. Notably, EU critical infrastructure
operators must complete their transition by the end of 2030.
The relative positions of global competitors in the so-called “quantum race” provide another perspective
on the Quantum Threat Timeline that may interest cybersecurity professionals. Most surveyed experts
identify North America as the current leader in quantum computing. Many believe it will likely maintain
this leadership for the next five years, but they also see China as a potential leader by then. Meanwhile,
Europe is viewed as somewhat falling behind.
Overall, given the rapid progress in quantum computing, the insights from our surveys, the momentum
from substantial investments in the field, and the threat from HNDL attacks, we cannot but conclude
that cybersecurity leaders and managers should act promptly to ensure cyber resilience in the face of
the looming quantum threat. They might do so by actively pursuing cryptographic modernization,
accelerating plans for a secure transition to PQC, and developing layered defenses against current and
future threats to public-key cryptography. Even organizations not yet officially required to adopt PQC
can benefit from a proactive stance, reducing the risk of rushing to quantum-safe cryptographic systems
later.

1 Introduction
This is the 7th report in an annual series that began in 2019, intended to shed light on when a quantum
computer powerful enough to be cryptographically relevant might be developed. The reports primarily
rely on a recurring survey of leading experts in quantum computing research. These experts have been
asked questions to evaluate the progress and pace of development in the field, and, most importantly,
to offer their best estimates of when such a cryptographically relevant quantum computer (CRQC) may
become available.
This introduction provides a brief background to help the reader understand how quantum computers
threaten cybersecurity and the significant scientific and technological challenges involved in developing
such computers. Some more technical information is offered via `in-depth` text boxes, and further
details can be found in the Appendix, which also contains a Glossary.

1.1 Quantum computing
Quantum mechanics is our most successful scientific framework for
understanding the behavior of systems such as atoms and fundamental
particles, including electrons and photons.

Quantum computers
leverage unique
quantum phenomena
to process
information in a
manner radically
different from that of
classical computers.

Understanding and controlling this behavior has been crucial for the
development of many modern technologies, including lasers and
computer microchips, during the 20th century. In recent decades,
researchers have realized that quantum features can be further exploited
at new qualitative and quantitative levels, redefining the limits of what is
technologically possible. With these efforts underway and already
producing remarkable results, the United Nations designated 2025 as "The International Year of
Quantum Science and Technology." Fittingly, the 2025 Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded “for the
discovery of macroscopic quantum mechanical tunneling and energy quantization in an electric circuit,"
with the additional highlight that such a discovery “has provided opportunities for developing the next
generation of quantum technology, including quantum cryptography, quantum computers, and
quantum sensors."
The primary obstacle in
advancing quantum
computing is the
unprecedented
requirement to maintain
and control quantum
behaviour at a level that
has never been
attempted before in
human history.

While we all benefit from the technological advancements that
quantum mechanics has enabled and continues to enable, quantum
theory describes the world in terms that may seem counterintuitive or
even absurd to the layperson. For example, a quantum system may
exist in a so-called superposition of states that would be mutually
exclusive in classical physics. One likely reason why quantum
phenomena tend to evade classical intuition is that quantum effects are
not apparent in everyday experience. This occurs because of the
physical scales involved and, most importantly, because quantum
phenomena are highly sensitive and easily disrupted by environmental
interactions, which can degrade or even effectively eliminate quantum
properties through a process known as decoherence.

9|Page

QUANTUM THREAT TIMELINE REPORT 2025

In classical computing, information is stored in bits
representing binary values, either "0" or "1." Quantum
computing seeks to leverage quantum phenomena such as
superposition to allow more sophisticated information
processing and to aid the simulation and understanding of
quantum systems. The basic unit of quantum information in
quantum computing is the qubit. Unlike a traditional bit
that holds a 0 or 1, a qubit can be in a superposition of both
values, allowing them to "coexist" and, roughly speaking, be
processed at the same time.
The monumental challenge in quantum computing research
and development is to mitigate and counteract
decoherence, protect fragile quantum features, and control
quantum systems with high precision to execute quantum
algorithms. Quantum error correction (QEC) is crucial for
encoding quantum information into more robust logical
qubits distributed across multiple inherently imperfect
physical qubits, enabling reliable information processing.

IN-DEPTH: IMPLEMENTING
QUANTUM COMPUTERS
Several proposals and approaches
are currently being developed to
build a quantum computer. They vary
in the physical substrate used—such
as superconducting circuits, trapped
ions, or quantum optics—and in the
specific degrees of freedom that
define qubits—for example, atomic
energy levels or so-called quantum
spin. They also differ in the
techniques used to implement
quantum error correction (QEC),
aiming to achieve so-called fault
tolerance (Nielsen and Chuang 2000).

A key milestone on the path toward a quantum computer has been demonstrating that QEC schemes
allow surpassing the so-called “break-even” condition, meaning that an encoded logical qubit performs
better than the physical qubits it is ‘made from’. By using enough physical qubits per logical qubit, it is in
principle possible to reduce logical errors to an arbitrarily low level. One major hurdle is that QEC
requires significant overhead in terms of physical qubits per logical qubit encoded and in the complexity
of physically implementing continuous error correction. This makes it daunting to scale the current
quantum computing devices to quantum computers with many logical qubits.
No fundamental barrier
to creating a large
quantum computer is
known, and such an
achievement should be
considered a matter of
“when” and “how” rather
than of “if”.

Nonetheless, no fundamental barrier to creating a large quantum
computer is known, and such an achievement should be considered a
matter of “when” and “how” rather than of “if”. Once realized,
quantum computers will not only fulfill Richard Feynman's vision of
efficiently simulating quantum systems (Feynman 1982) but also, by
cleverly leveraging quantum features like superposition via specialized
algorithms, solve a range of mathematical, optimization, and search
problems at speeds unattainable by classical computers (Nielsen and
Chuang 2000).

1.2 Quantum threat to cybersecurity
The emergence of quantum computing introduces the risk of severe failures in current cyber-systems,
both through direct attacks using quantum cryptanalysis and by undermining trust in these systems.
The looming threat posed by a Cryptographically Relevant Quantum Computer (CRQC) can be addressed
through the implementation of quantum-resistant cryptographic techniques, which may be either
classical or quantum-based. The former includes cryptographic algorithms based on problems that are

considered difficult even for quantum computers. In 2024, the U.S. National Institute of Standards and
Technology (NIST) issued the first standards for Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) (NIST 2024).
Additionally, tools like quantum key distribution (QKD) leverage quantum principles to establish shared
secret keys for secure communication (Nielsen and Chuang, 2000). Transitioning to this new breed of
quantum-safe cryptography is a complex and delicate process (Mosca 2013): it requires the
development and deployment of hardware and software solutions, the establishment and adoption of
standards, the migration of legacy systems, and more.
With the necessity to devote enough time to an orderly and safe transition to a ‘post-quantum world’,
the urgency for any organization to complete the transition to quantum-safe cryptography for a
particular cyber-system can be determined by considering three simple but important parameters1
(Mosca 2013):
TSHELF-LIFE (shelf-life time):

the number of years the information should be protected for;

TMIGRATION (migration time): the number of years needed to safely and adequately migrate the cybersystem to a quantum-safe solution;
TTHREAT (threat time):

the number of years before the relevant threat actors will be able to break
the quantum-vulnerable systems, an event often referred to as “Q-Day”.

If TSHELF-LIFE + TMIGRATION > TTHREAT, that is, if the time required
to migrate the system plus the time for which the
information needs to be protected goes beyond the time
when the quantum threat will become concrete, then an
organization may not be able to protect its assets for the
required TSHELF-LIFE years against the quantum threat (see
also Figure 1). This is the content of the Mosca Inequality
(Mosca 2013), and is due to the possibility of “Harvest
Now, Decrypt Later” attacks (Figure 5), which may affect
current data/messages even if a CRQC is not yet available.
Nonetheless, it is important to stress that the risk is not
simply exposed secrets. Suppose the quantum threat
arrives before quantum-safe migrations of systems are
completed. In that case, integrity, availability, and
operational control across critical systems are at stake –
that is, a systemic security and operational failure, not just
a confidentiality breach.

1.3 Quantum computing towards fault tolerance
It is currently believed that quantum cryptanalysis requires running quantum algorithms on a sufficiently
large quantum computer that employs quantum error correction. In general, such a fault-tolerant
quantum computer is needed for quantum algorithms developed to be run on an idealized error-free
quantum computer. It will still take time to reach such a stage.
Meanwhile, there is undeniable excitement about the practical and commercial potential of “earlystage” quantum computers, even if not yet advanced enough to run arbitrary quantum algorithms and
threaten cybersecurity. Such early computers may not adopt quantum error correction, but rather
techniques for the more limited—but less demanding in terms of hardware—quantum error mitigation.
The latter aims at managing errors and decoherence via clever circuit designs, repeated runs of the
quantum computation, and classical processing of data (Eisert and Preskill 2025).

1.3.1 From NISQ to FASQ
In the past years, there has been interest in so-called Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices
(Preskill 2018), with tens to hundreds of physical qubits. There have been demonstrations – often
controversial ones – of how such devices may go beyond what standard computers are capable of, the
most famous example being perhaps the realization of so-called “quantum supremacy” (Arute et al.
2019). The controversies surrounding these demonstrations usually deal with two aspects:
• the problems/tasks considered to prove a quantum advantage may be specifically designed with that
goal in mind, but not be of any practical utility;
• the claim of surpassing the capabilities of standard computers may lack the solidity of a
mathematical proof and consider only the best-known classical approach/algorithm, while a better
classical approach/algorithm may exist.
Regarding the second point, NISQ
computers are now sufficiently large

In assessing progress towards fault-tolerant computation, one can consider that logical computation
may be implemented/demonstrated at different levels of capability and functionality. Barmes et al.
(Barmes et al. 2025) suggested a three-level classification framework:
Level 1: Error correction of quantum memory, to preserve logically encoded quantum information
without any processing;
Level 2: Limited fault-tolerant logical operations that can be implemented relatively easily on the
encoded quantum information, for example, by acting in parallel on the underlying physical
qubits;
Level 3: Full set of fault-tolerant logical operations, requiring a significant overhead in terms of physical
qubits and complex active error-correction, but allowing one to run arbitrary (small-scale)
quantum algorithms at the logical level.
When evaluating experimental advances in error correction and fault-tolerant computation, particularly

KEY POINTS
• Quantum computing uses quantum mechanics principles to conduct specific calculations
more efficiently than traditional computers. The basic unit of information in a quantum
computer is called quantum bit, or qubit.
• A Cryptographically Relevant Quantum Computer (CRQC) is a quantum computer capable
of breaking many common cryptographic systems once it is built, representing a major
threat to data security.
• Building a CRQC requires implementing quantum error correction techniques to manage
and stabilize the inherently fragile quantum states that form logical qubits.
• Developing a CRQC presents an enormous scientific and engineering challenge, as it
requires both scaling the number of physical qubits and maintaining high-quality control
over them, making any estimates of a CRQC timeline inherently uncertain.
• To reduce the risks from a CRQC, it is essential to switch to quantum-safe cryptographic
tools. This process is complex and needs thorough planning to avoid creating new
vulnerabilities.
• The urgency of transitioning to quantum-safe cryptography depends on three
parameters: the projected timeline for a CRQC, the time required to migrate to quantumsafe cryptography, and the time the data needs protection for, also called shelf-life.
• If the time needed for migration, combined with the data's shelf-life, exceeds the
estimated timeline for the quantum threat, assets could become vulnerable. The socalled Mosca Inequality captures this idea and accounts for the “Harvest Now, Decrypt
Later” attacks.
• There is excitement about the application and commercial potential of “early-stage”
quantum computers, which are not yet advanced enough to pose a cybersecurity threat.
These “early” quantum computing applications could facilitate the development of a fullfledged CRQC.

2 Scope of this report
This document presents the findings of a survey conducted by evolutionQ Inc., involving 26
internationally recognized experts in quantum computing research and commercialization. Continuing
the tradition of similar surveys from the past six years, the experts were invited to complete an online
questionnaire about the current state of the field.
When designing the questionnaire, we emphasized clarity and specificity, especially when evaluating
quantum computers as a potential cybersecurity threat. Consequently, the main question directly
addresses breaking RSA-2048, whose security depends on the difficulty of factoring a 2048-bit number.
The main results focus on providing opinion-based likelihood estimates of when RSA-2048 might be
broken by a cryptographically relevant quantum computer (CRQC).
We aim to capture a snapshot of the experts' opinions and identify potential trends in how they change
over time. Such trends can be appreciated by comparing the results of the multiple surveys that we have
run year after year. In general, shifts in opinions—both at the level of individual participants who have
consistently taken part in our surveys and at the level of the overall pool of respondents—may stem
from steady advancements in quantum computing research, unexpected developments or challenges,
and external factors like funding and investment levels. We try to capture the significance of all these
influences with additional targeted questions.
Notably, we also report on the milestones the experts consider relevant to monitor in the near-tomedium term.

2.1 Related literature
Our approach centers on providing likelihood estimates based on expert opinions. Additional
information and resources are available that are directly or indirectly related to estimating the timeline
for developing a fault-tolerant quantum computer that could threaten cybersecurity.
For instance, Sevilla and Riedel (Sevilla and Riedel 2020) attempt to predict future progress in quantum

roughly speaking, the number of effective logical qubits available for computation. Sevilla and Riedel
focus on superconducting implementations and, like our approach, on breaking RSA-2048. The authors
describe their estimates for when superconducting quantum computers might achieve this as “one
piece of relevant evidence that can supplement expert opinion” and “more pessimistic but broadly
comparable to those produced through the survey of experts” in the first of our series of reports. Their
paper is based on data up to 2020 and has not been further updated.
Firms like McKinsey & Company are monitoring the development of quantum technologies (McKinsey &
Company 2025). Their analysis is based on various sources, including publicly available data on
investment levels and the number of publications and patent applications, as well as expert interviews
and proprietary McKinsey analyses. Their forecasts aim at assessing the growth and future size of the
market for quantum technologies, including quantum computing and quantum communication. Like
Sevilla and Riedel, they also extrapolate from past progress in quantum computing to arrive at a range
of dates for “Q-Day”, but do so less rigorously, as estimating the quantum threat timeline is not the
central goal of their report.

4.3 Most promising scheme for fault-tolerance
Fault-tolerance will be achieved through a combination of enhanced
hardware performance and an appropriate error-correction scheme

Correspondingly, the responses of the experts were relatively nuanced,
highlighting, for example, that the choice of error-correction code may
vary by architecture. Such a dependency arises from factors such as
ALEXANDER BLAIS
bias in the kinds of errors the underlying physical qubits may
University of Sherbrooke
experience, or the connectivity, that is, whether direct interactions
between the physical qubits are limited to nearest neighbours or can
also be long-range. Even the same architecture may use different codes depending on the tasks – for
example, memory vs computation.
Some variation of the surface code is still considered a leading choice for superconducting
implementations, but quantum Low-Density Parity-Check (qLDPC) codes (see Appendix) are actively
being developed and considered very promising, if not the future, by some. This is because such codes
improve the encoding rate, meaning fewer physical qubits are needed per logical qubit. One price to pay
is the need for relatively long-range interactions.
Along these lines, Joe Fitzsimons, CEO of Horizon Quantum Computing, writes:
There are still research questions to be answered, so I would be hesitant to say that we know what the
encoding [likely to drive the future evolution of fault-tolerant architectures] will be at present, but there
is clearly a lot of progress being made on quantum LDPC codes.
Other proposals for fault tolerance that aim to reduce error-correction overhead are based on encoding
quantum information into physical qubits that are inherently protected against certain types of error,
such as qubits encoded in states of harmonic oscillators – so-called bosonic qubits. For such qubits,
error-correction schemes can focus on the remaining error types.
Nicolas Menicucci explains why he sees bosonic codes as “the most promising scheme on paper”, while
acknowledging that turning them into a working fault-tolerant architecture still requires some serious
technological breakthrough:
Encoding a qubit in a single bosonic mode [..] is conceptually attractive because it lets you
concentrate redundancy inside one well-controlled oscillator instead of spreading it over a large

array of bare qubits. [..] If we could reliably prepare and stabilise good bosonic code states [in
superconducting architectures], they would make very natural “level-0” qubits on top of which
more conventional stabiliser codes could sit.
[In some photonic proposals,] bosonic codes would live in optical modes rather than microwave
cavities. There, the appeal is different – room-temperature operation and better prospects for

[T]he core challenge is similar: we do not yet know how to make these encoded bosonic qubits

When it comes to physical implementations suitable for fault-tolerant quantum computing, Lieven

literally the weak links.”

KEY POINTS
• Research on quantum error-correction is a highly active field and could see significant
breakthrough results.
• The choice of error-correction code may vary by architecture, based on, for example, the
underlying physical errors or the kind of connectivity.
• Some variation of the surface code is still considered a leading choice for superconducting
implementations.
• Quantum Low-Density Parity-Check (qLDPC) codes are actively being developed and
adapted to hardware, because they reduce encoding overhead and facilitate scaling. They
require long-range connectivity, though, which makes them not immediately suitable for
all physical implementations.
• Bosonic codes also hold promise to reduce encoding overhead and facilitate scaling, but

4.7 Current progress
In this section, we present opinions about the status of progress in quantum computing research and
development.

4.7.1 Recent developments
The latter half of 2024 and early 2025 have witnessed a surge in the achievement of experimental
milestones, particularly in the performance of error-corrected logical qubits compared to their physical
counterparts. Respondents emphasized that the field is moving beyond simple break-even
demonstrations toward robust error suppression 9.
A major highlight identified by multiple experts is the advancement in superconducting architectures,
specifically Google’s results with the "Willow" processor (Acharya et al. 2025). Experts noted that this
processor has demonstrated quantum advantage and, crucially, performance beyond the break-even
point for error correction. One respondent detailed that the processor realized distance-5 and distance7 surface-code memories, with the distance-7 logical qubit surviving more than twice as long as the best
physical qubit.
Beyond superconducting circuits, significant progress was noted in other implementations.
Experts described progress in neutral atoms as "truly spectacular," particularly the activities surrounding
Rydberg atom arrays (see, for example, (Bluvstein et al. 2025; Manetsch et al. 2025)). However, it was
noted that the "very small clock rate" remains a challenge for running complex algorithms on this
platform. The progress in error correction and logical computation in ion-trap platforms was also
considered a recent experimental highlight (see, for example, (Daguerre et al. 2025)).
The community reacted positively to the experimental concatenation of bosonic cat qubits in a
repetition code (Putterman et al. 2025). This was highlighted as an excellent example of exploiting noise
bias to create a pathway for low-overhead quantum error correction.
On the theoretical side, experts pointed to (Gidney 2025) as a candidate for the most significant
achievement, noting it reduces the number of qubits required to factor a 2048-bit number to below 1
million. This makes the timeline for cryptographically relevant quantum computers more plausible than
estimates from several years ago. Another work highlighted as significant along these lines is
(Chevignard et al. 2025).

4.7.2 Next near-term step
When asked what essential steps should be achieved by roughly Summer 2026, the consensus shifted
from establishing logical memory to demonstrating logical computation and scalability.
A key milestone would be a programmable logical processor with 5–10 logical qubits capable of
executing a universal gate set—including fault-tolerant non-Clifford gates—that outperforms unencoded
implementations.

Summary and outlook
A quantum computer capable of effectively running quantum cryptanalysis algorithms poses a threat to
widely adopted cryptosystems that rely on computational problems believed to be intractable for
current computers but relatively easy for a cryptographically relevant quantum computer (CRQC).
Building a CRQC will require years of progress in both science and engineering, which can only be
achieved through dedicated commitment and sufficient resources. The main challenge to address is the
inherent ‘fragility’ of the quantum features that give quantum computing its advantage over classical
computing. The end goal of quantum computing research is computations that use logical qubits, a
dependable way to encode and handle quantum information, even when the underlying physical qubits
are prone to errors.
The expert opinions collected and summarized in this report offer unique insights into the timeline of
the quantum threat. Twenty-six respondents estimated the likelihood of a quantum computer capable
of breaking a scheme like RSA-2048 within 24 hours – our working notion of a CRQC – and shared their
opinions on various aspects of quantum computing development and on diverse factors influencing it.
The past year has seen several experimental demonstrations of quantum error correction, logical
encoding, and logical computation that our respondents generally find impressive. Alongside advances
in error-mitigation and correction techniques, increases in the number of physical qubits across
platforms, and the theoretical development of efficient error-correction schemes, these achievements
support an optimistic outlook for the future of quantum computing.
Recent advancements have been so significant that this year’s opinions are the most optimistic for the
“within 10 years” timeframe that we have ever recorded in our series of surveys: half of the respondents

Physical realizations
The various physical implementations of quantum computers have advantages and disadvantages in
relation to factors such as (but not limited to):
•
•
•
•

scalability, that is, the possibility of building and controlling large quantum devices with more and

The following is a very high-level classification of some physical realizations:
•

•
•

•
•

•

Quantum optics, meaning that information is stored and manipulated in states of light; this includes
polarization states or photon-number states, and can be implemented also on-chip by using
integrated optics.
Superconducting systems, meaning that information is stored and manipulated in electric circuits
that exploit the properties of superconducting materials.
Topological systems, meaning that information is stored and manipulated in some topological
properties—that is, properties that depend on ‘global’ (geometric) properties insensitive to ‘local’
changes—of quantum systems.
Ion traps, meaning that information is stored and manipulated in properties of ions (atoms with
non-vanishing total electric charge) that are confined by electro-magnetic fields.
Quantum spin systems, meaning that information is stored and manipulated in the internal degree
of freedom called quantum spin; such systems may be realized in silicon, like standard microchips
are, or in less conventional systems, like diamonds with point defects known as nitrogen-vacancy (or
NV, in short) centers.
Cold atoms, where neutral atoms (rather than ions) are cooled down to close to absolute zero.
While ions repel each other because of their electric charge, neutral atoms do not and can be
trapped and arranged in very regular arrays via the use of laser beams that generate so-called
optical lattices; the atoms can then be controlled all the way down to the level of individual sites in
the lattice.

Error correction, fault tolerance, and logical qubits
Errors and imperfections in the manipulation of (quantum) information, as well as decoherence, may be
reduced by improving the physical implementation, including qubit control, but they cannot be entirely
eliminated. Nonetheless, reliable storage and processing of quantum can still be achieved by employing
error correction schemes: logical qubits are encoded into multiple physical qubits, so that errors
affecting the underlying physical qubits can be detected and corrected, and logical information be
protected. Error correction can ultimately lead to fault tolerance (Nielsen and Chuang 2000): under
reasonable assumptions, one can prove that, if the error rate of the underlying physical components is
low enough—below the so-called fault-tolerance threshold—then it is possible to implement logical
encodings for information and information processing that can be made arbitrarily reliable, at the cost
of using a number of physical qubits that is potentially much larger than that of the encoded logical
qubits, but that still scales in a manageable way, at least theoretically.
Some more details on such codes and techniques can be found below. Still, they are not as relevant as
keeping in mind that quantum error correction and fault-tolerance do pave the way to digital quantum
computers: in principle, quantum computing devices can be made as reliable as needed, once some
“quality standard” and some scalability & integration of the underlying physical qubits are achieved. We
provide information on some specific error-correcting codes to 1) facilitate the understanding of the
expert opinions on the topic and 2) to make it clear that developing codes that enable fault tolerance,
also considering their ease of realization and tailoring them to specific physical implementation, is an
on-going and very important area of research. Most relevantly, improvements in error-correcting codes
and/or in their hardware implementation may speed up the quantum threat timeline.

Examples of error correcting codes
Surface codes, which are an instance of so-called topological quantum error correcting codes (Kitaev
2003), are currently among the leading candidates for large-scale quantum error correction. The surface
code (Fowler et al. 2012) allows for the detection and correction of errors on a two-dimensional array of
nearest-neighbour coupled physical qubits via repeatedly measuring two types of so-called stabilizers
generators. A single logical qubit is encoded into a square array of physical qubits. A classical error
detection algorithm must be run at regular intervals (surface code cycle) to track the propagation of
physical qubit errors and, ultimately, to prevent logical errors. Every surface code cycle involves some
number of one- and two-qubit physical quantum gates, physical qubit measurements, and classical
processing to detect and correct errors (i.e., decoding). Surface codes can provide logical qubits with
lower overall error rates, at a price of increasing the number of physical qubits per logical qubit – that is,
the size of the square array – and the cost of decoding.
Low-Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes have widespread use in the handling of classical information, as
they have an essentially optimal scaling in terms of rate of encoding—the ratio between reliable logical
bits and underlying faulty bits. Significant effort has recently been put into researching good quantum
LDPC codes, which are characterized by the constraint that the number of underlying physical qubits
involved in each error check and the number of checks each qubit is involved in are bounded by a
constant (Breuckmann and Eberhardt 2021). One challenge with quantum LDPC codes is that the qubits
used in the encoding and in the error correction, despite being “few”, may be far apart.
Bosonic codes constitute a major class of quantum error-correcting codes, alongside surface codes and
quantum LDPC codes. Instead of encoding a logical qubit into many two-level systems, a bosonic code
encodes it into one or a few harmonic oscillators—for example, a microwave cavity mode in a
superconducting circuit or an optical mode. This encoding can be designed so that the main physical
noise, such as single-photon loss or phase noise, is relatively straightforward to detect and correct.
Notable examples include “cat codes” (Cochrane et al. 1999), where superpositions of coherent states
encode logical 0 and 1, and Gottesman–Kitaev–Preskill (GKP) codes (Gottesman et al. 2001), where
logical states are superpositions of position- and momentum-eigenstates arranged on a lattice in phase
space. A key advantage of bosonic codes is their ability to embed some error protection directly into the
physical degree of freedom, lowering the reliance on higher-level quantum error correction. However,
bosonic codes also present challenges; for instance, preparing and manipulating cat states or GKP states
require high-fidelity control and often complex hardware.

A.5 Glossary
This glossary provides short definitions of key terms and concepts related to quantum computing and
cryptography as discussed in this report.
Superposition
The phenomenon in which the state of a quantum system can comprise alternatives that would be
mutually exclusive in classical physics.
Qubit
The basic unit of quantum information. Unlike a classical bit that can be 0 or 1, a qubit can exist in a
superposition of both states simultaneously, enabling quantum computers to process information in
fundamentally different ways.
Logical Qubit
A robust unit of quantum information encoded across multiple physical qubits using error-correction
techniques to mitigate noise and decoherence. Logical qubits are essential for fault-tolerant quantum
computing.
Physical Qubit
An actual hardware implementation of a qubit, which is inherently imperfect and prone to errors.
Multiple physical qubits are combined to form a logical qubit.
Entanglement
A uniquely quantum correlation in which two or more quantum systems become linked so that the state
of one cannot be described independently of the others.
Decoherence
The process by which quantum systems lose their uniquely quantum properties, such as superposition,
due to interactions with their surrounding environment.
Quantum Error Correction (QEC)
A set of techniques that protect quantum information by encoding it into logical qubits distributed
across multiple physical qubits. QEC is critical for achieving fault tolerance in quantum computing.
Fault Tolerance
The ability of a quantum computer to continue operating correctly even when some components fail or
experience errors, achieved through QEC and robust architectures.
Cryptographically Relevant Quantum Computer (CRQC)
A quantum computer powerful enough to break widely used cryptographic systems, such as RSA-2048,
within practical timeframes.

73 | P a g e

QUANTUM THREAT TIMELINE REPORT 2025

Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC)
Cryptographic algorithms designed to be secure against attacks by quantum computers. PQC includes
lattice-based, hash-based, and other schemes standardized by NIST.
Harvest Now, Decrypt Later (HNDL)
An attack strategy where adversaries intercept and store encrypted data today, intending to decrypt it
in the future when quantum computers become powerful enough to break current encryption.
Mosca Inequality
A principle stating that, if the time until the quantum threat is less than the sum of the data shelf-life
and migration time, then sensitive data may become vulnerable to quantum-enabled breaches.
Shelf-Life Time
The number of years information must remain secure.
Migration Time
The time required to safely transition systems to quantum-safe cryptography.
Quantum Threat Time
The estimated time until adversaries have access to quantum computers capable of breaking current
cryptographic systems.
Q-Day
The moment when quantum computers become sufficiently powerful to compromise current
cryptographic standards, rendering many widely used encryption methods vulnerable.
NISQ (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum)
Quantum devices with tens to hundreds of physical qubits, not yet fault-tolerant, but potentially useful
for specific, specialized tasks.
FASQ (Fault-Tolerant Application-Scale Quantum)
A future class of quantum computers capable of running large-scale, error-corrected computations,
including cryptanalysis.
Quantum LDPC (Low-Density Parity-Check) Codes
Quantum error-correcting codes that improve encoding efficiency, reducing the number of physical
qubits needed per logical qubit. They may require relatively long-distance connectivity between qubits.
Surface Code
A quantum error-correcting code that encodes logical qubits into a two-dimensional array of nearestneighbor coupled physical qubits.
Bosonic Codes
A class of quantum error-correcting codes that encode logical qubits into the states of harmonic
